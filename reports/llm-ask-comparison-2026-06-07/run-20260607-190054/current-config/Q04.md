Sparse vectors are useful for rare or specialized terms because they represent text with dimensions tied to words or subwords and weight the important tokens, which preserves the ability to query exact words and phrases. [S1] Qdrant’s article says they “shine” when many rare keywords or specialized terms are present, such as medical-domain text, where rare terms may be missing from general vocabulary and general-purpose dense vectors may not capture the domain nuances. [S1]

They also provide efficiency and interpretability benefits: most values are zero, only non-zero dimensions need to be stored, and token weights can help explain why a document matched a query. [S1][S3] In hybrid search, sparse vectors can help ensure that results containing required keywords are returned, while dense vectors cover semantically similar results. [S1]

Their main weakness is semantic nuance: sparse vectors are not as good as dense vectors at capturing relationships between words, such as the relationship between “king” and “queen.” [S1] Dense vectors are described as “meaning-rich” embeddings that capture nuanced relationships more strongly, while sparse vectors focus on key lexical features and exact or weighted token matching. Sparse vectors are useful for rare or specialized terms because they represent text with dimensions tied to words or subwords and weight the important tokens, which preserves the ability to query exact words and phrases. [S1] Qdrant’s article says they “shine” when many rare keywords or specialized terms are present, such as medical-domain text, where rare terms may be missing from general vocabulary and general-purpose dense vectors may not capture the domain nuances. They also provide efficiency and interpretability benefits: most values are zero, only non-zero dimensions need to be stored, and token weights can help explain why a document matched a query. [S1][S2] In hybrid search, sparse vectors can help ensure that results containing required keywords are returned, while dense vectors cover semantically similar results. [S1]

Their main weakness is semantic nuance: sparse vectors are not as good as dense vectors at capturing relationships between words, such as the relationship between “king” and “queen.” [S1] Dense vectors are described as “meaning-rich” embeddings that capture nuanced relationships more strongly, while sparse vectors focus on key lexical features and exact or weighted token matching.
