Sparse vectors are useful for rare or specialized terms because they focus on relative word weights per document, making them excel in domains where many rare keywords or specialized terms are present, such as the medical domain, where general-purpose dense vectors might fail to capture domain nuances [S1]. This approach allows them to maintain the capability of querying exact words and phrases, which is crucial for information retrieval, mirroring the functionality of methods like BM25 [S1]. Furthermore, advanced methods using sparse vectors, like SPLADE, can perform "term expansion," suggesting contextually relevant terms not explicitly mentioned in the original text [S1].

Compared to dense vectors, sparse vectors fall short in their ability to capture nuanced relationships between words; for instance, they struggle to model the relationship between concepts like "king" and "queen" as effectively as dense vectors do [S1]. While they provide interpretability by showing which specific tokens contribute most to the relevance score, dense vectors are generally considered more robust for capturing deep semantic meaning and complex relationships [S1].
