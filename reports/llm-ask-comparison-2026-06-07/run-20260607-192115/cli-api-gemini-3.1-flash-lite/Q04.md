Qdrant’s documentation highlights that sparse vectors are particularly useful for rare or specialized terms—such as those in the medical domain—where general-purpose dense vectors may lack the necessary nuances [S1]. Because sparse vectors focus on exact lexical matching for specific keywords or tokens, they excel in scenarios where capturing the precise presence of a term is essential [S2][S3].

However, sparse vectors fall short of dense vectors in several key areas:
* **Nuanced Relationships:** Sparse vectors struggle to capture the underlying meaning or context of words and the complex relationships between them [S1]. For instance, they are less effective than dense vectors at understanding the relationship between concepts like "king" and "queen" [S1].
* **Contextual Comprehension:** Traditional sparse methods (like BM25) rely on word frequency and do not attempt to comprehend the contextual importance or meaning of the words within a document [S1].
* **Generalization:** Because dense vectors are "meaning-rich," they are better at capturing semantic similarities and nuances across the entire dataset, whereas sparse vectors are strictly limited to the presence of specific tokens [S1][S3].

To address these respective limitations, modern search systems often employ **hybrid search**, which combines the precision of sparse vectors for keyword matching with the semantic richness of dense vectors [S1][S2][S3].
